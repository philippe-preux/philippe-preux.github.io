Motivations

My motivation for doing my job, and before that, being a student and aiming at becoming a researcher, is mostly a search for understanding things, and think about things. And sharing this with other people, colleagues, students, whoever might be interested in, together going further towards new unknown territories.

I am not really interested in digging further, rather in finding new areas to dig into. I prefer exploring new areas, than exploiting.
This led me to change my field of interest very strongy along the years and visit various remote areas of computer science: symbolic AI, computer architecture, languages and compilers, genetic algorithms, combinatorial optimization, multi-agent systems, machine learning, data mining, numerical/statistical AI.
This also led me to work with researchers of other fields: marine biologists, behaviorist psychologists, agronomists, health researchers.
This also led me to collaborate with companies on various topics to understand companies point of view, needs, etc.

Research activities and projects

In this section, I describe my current research activities and projects.
Fundamentally, my research domain is machine learning, and particularly sequential decision making under unceertainty, mostly reinforcement learning, a bit of bandits, and a bit of any other relevant topic.
I have decided a couple of years ago to focus on a set of research topics and applications of my research. After 30 years of research activities, I feel like I need to try to make my research useful, at least to some extent, to the well-being of society, humankind, and Life on earth (!). This led me to select the set of research questions I want to investigate in the coming years. Regarding applications, I want to focus on health, and sustainable development.
I think that machine learning has brought new tools, algorithms, methods, ... that have to be used, put to the test, and tailored to important fields of applications. Applications also bring questions and challenges that have to be dealt with by fundamental research, and drawing my attention to issues that are meaningfull.

Research: general topics

In this section, I say a few words about the directions in which I already work or I want to work in the coming years.

  • generalization in RL.
    An RL agent having been trained to solve one, or a couple, of tasks, how can it solve other, related, tasks? This question encompasses transfer learning, but goes far beyond.
  • non stationary combinatorial optimization problems with uncertainty.
  • combining numerical learning and symbolic learning.

To sum-up my thinking about so-called AI, there is absolutely nothing intelligent in it: current AI systems, sometimes achieving remarkable feats, are completely stupid, and rely exclusively on computation power, and the smartness of their designers and implementors. I want to go further than that. There are numerous tasks where current state-of-the-art AI is simply unable to do anyhting or, if it can, does it in a completely stupid way, a completely different way from the one we, as human beings, use.

Past activities

In this section, I very briefly describe the topics I worked in the past.

  • in symbolic AI: mostly in the 1980's, at the glorious time of expert systems, I was very interested in AI languages (mostly Lisp and Smalltalk and a bit of Prolog) for knowledge representation and reasoning.
  • computer architecture (1990-1992): part of my PhD thesis is dedicated to proposing to execute vector operations in an out-of-order way, in order to reduce contentions due to dependencies. This idea was later developed further in T. Kechadi's PhD
  • Languages and compilers (1988-1992): during my PhD and a bit afterwards, we designed various languages and language constructs to express vector operations instead of the traditionnal loop expression. I was mostly concerned by the low level part: I designed a vector virtual machine and its vector intermediate language, and I implemented an interpreter of this language on Sun workstation, and Cray Y-MP.
  • genetic algorithm (1993-1999): I studied he dynamics of the population of a genetic algorithm. I read a lot about the theory of natural selection in biology, and I was able to observe many aspects of the dynamics of population in genetic algorithms.
  • combinatorial optimization (1995-1999): I studied the notion of optimization landscape of a couple of combinatorial optimization problems, in particular the TSP. I had in mind to use simple hill-climbing algorithms to study the topology of this landscape, better understand the behavior of such search algorithms, trying to understand what makes an instance easy or hard to solve.
  • multi-agent systems (1997-1999): collaborating with marine biologists, we design a MAS to investigate the hypothesis that the copepod is actively looking for food, not just waiting that food flows in its mouth.
  • reinforcement learning (1994-2004): I spent many years collaborating with psychologists to understand how animal behavior is acquired, how it adapts to its environment, ... This eventually led me to reinforcement learning.
  • graphs: as a computer scientist, I see graphs everywhere. I have investigated Markov decision problems considered as graphs, evolving graphs, graph representation, graph generation, combinatorial optimization problems related to graphs, and many other aspects of graphs.
  • machine learning for software development (2010-2018): through a couple of collaborations, I have investigated how machine learning can be used to help software development. Graphs are involved either as the control graph of a sofwtare, or as an artefact hierarchy, or other graphs that are natural representations of software artefacts.
  • reinforcement learning (since 2000): I have also studied RL as a computer scientist, investigating algorithms to solve this problem, applying them to solve real/realistic problems, ...
  • machine learning (since 2000): I have studied various aspects of machine learning. LARS and sparse function approximators (to be used in RL); sparse matrix factorization; data which attributes are functions; random matrix theory.
  • bandits (2009-2017): recommendation systems; computational advertising; real function optimization
